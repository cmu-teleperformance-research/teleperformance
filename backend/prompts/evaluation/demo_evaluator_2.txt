=== COMPONENT 1: TASK COMPLETION ===

Does the CSR's current message make a concrete contribution toward resolving the customer's problem — confirming the specific problem, asking a question that gathers needed information, or taking/explaining a correct action per the scenario procedure?

- Strong: A clear, correct contribution — pins down the specific problem, asks a targeted diagnostic question, or takes a correct action with verifiable specifics (what, when, what happens next).
- Adequate: Relevant but partial — a generic question, a vague next step, or a correct action missing specifics, ownership, or expectations.
- Developing: No forward progress — off-target, incorrect, repeats what is known, asks for information already provided, proceeds on assumptions, contradicts scenario policy, or purely procedural filler.

Routine verification requests (name, booking reference, ID) earn at most Adequate. Content that is relevant and correct but incomplete — including stopping or closing too early — is Adequate, not Developing; reserve Developing for content that is off-target, incorrect, or adds nothing. Empathy, apology, or acknowledgment wording ("I am sorry to hear that") is never a task contribution — if the message contains only emotional or conversational wording, the quote is "". If the quote contains relevant, correct content, the rating is at least Adequate — regardless of what the message failed to do, and regardless of the Emotion rating.

=== COMPONENT 2: EMOTION HANDLING ===

Does the CSR's current message respond appropriately to the customer's emotional state? Identify that state from the customer's message BEFORE the CSR's current message (frustration, urgency, anxiety, anger, distrust, confusion, relief, neutral, etc.). The state calibrates expectations; it is not itself rated.

When the customer shows negative emotion (frustration, urgency, anxiety, anger, distrust, confusion, or unresolved dissatisfaction):
- Strong: Specifically acknowledges the emotion or situation in the CSR's own words (not a stock phrase), sounds human and non-defensive, then pivots to the task.
- Adequate: Contains explicit acknowledgment wording (e.g., "I'm sorry...", "I understand...", naming the customer's concern or feeling), but it is generic, delayed, or only partly matched. A generic apology ("I am sorry to hear that") is Adequate at best, never Strong.
- Developing: Contains no explicit acknowledgment wording at all; or ignores clear emotion; sounds cold, dismissive, defensive, or rushed; uses empathy as a rebuttal; or pushes toward task or closing while emotion is unresolved.

Acknowledgment must be visible wording in the CSR's message that addresses the concern or emotion the customer actually stated. To enforce this, fill "acknowledgment_quote" BEFORE the emotion rating: copy the exact qualifying wording from the CSR's current message (and only that message), or "" if none. Two kinds of wording do NOT qualify and must not be copied: (a) status updates, information, or facts — being on-topic or useful is not acknowledgment ("Your order shows as shipped and should arrive Friday" is a status update, not acknowledgment); (b) wording that fabricates or misreads the customer's stated concern (e.g., sympathizing about a concern the customer explicitly said they do not have) — mismatched empathy is not acknowledgment. Generic wording that does not misread the concern ("I am sorry to hear that") DOES qualify — copy it; it caps the rating at Adequate. If the customer shows negative emotion and acknowledgment_quote is "", the rating MUST be Developing. If the customer is calm, neutral, or cooperative, an empty acknowledgment_quote is normal and expected — it does NOT lower the rating; apply the calm-customer rule below instead. Never describe an information-only or mismatched message as having "acknowledged" the customer's concern. Accurate reflection of the customer's concern and stakes supports a higher rating; wording that only partly matches the concern cannot be Strong.

When the customer is calm, neutral, or cooperative: do not demand explicit empathy. Professional warmth earns Adequate; Strong requires genuinely skilled emotional work (e.g., proactively reassuring about an earlier concern); Developing only if the tone is cold, dismissive, or mishandles the customer.

Emotion feedback: one sentence, max 35 words, second person ("you", "your"). If Developing or Adequate: the single most important thing to do differently, specific to this turn and customer. If Strong: the strength to maintain. Interpersonal handling only — acknowledgment, tone, wording, timing — never procedural steps.
