=== COMPONENT 1: TASK COMPLETION ===

Does the CSR's current message make a concrete contribution toward resolving the customer's problem — accurately confirming the specific problem, asking a question that gathers needed information, or taking/explaining a correct action consistent with the scenario procedure?

- Strong: A clear, correct contribution — pins down the specific problem, asks a targeted diagnostic question, or takes a correct action with verifiable specifics (what, when, what happens next).
- Adequate: Relevant but partial — a generic question, a vague next step, or a correct action missing specifics, ownership, or expectations.
- Developing: No forward progress — off-target, repeats what is known, asks for information already provided, proceeds on assumptions, contradicts scenario policy, or purely procedural filler.

Routine verification requests (name, booking reference, ID) do not by themselves earn more than Adequate.

A message whose content is relevant and correct but incomplete, or that stops or closes too early, is Adequate, not Developing. Reserve Developing for messages whose content itself is off-target, incorrect, or adds nothing. === COMPONENT 2: EMOTION HANDLING ===

Does the CSR's current message respond appropriately to the customer's emotional state? Identify that state from the customer's message BEFORE the CSR's current message (frustration, urgency, anxiety, anger, distrust, confusion, relief, neutral, etc.). The state calibrates expectations; it is not itself rated.

When the customer shows frustration, urgency, anxiety, anger, distrust, confusion, or unresolved dissatisfaction:
- Strong: Specifically acknowledges the emotion or situation in the CSR's own words (not a stock phrase), sounds human and non-defensive, then pivots to the task.
- Adequate: Contains explicit acknowledgment wording (e.g., "I'm sorry...", "I understand...", naming the customer's concern or feeling), but it is generic, delayed, or only partly matched to the customer's specific concern. A generic apology ("I am sorry to hear that") is Adequate at best, never Strong.
- Developing: Contains no explicit acknowledgment wording at all; or ignores clear emotion; sounds cold, dismissive, defensive, or rushed; uses empathy as a rebuttal; or pushes toward task or closing while emotion is unresolved.

Acknowledgment must be visible wording in the CSR's message. Being on-topic, answering the question, or providing useful information is not acknowledgment — e.g., "I can see your bag was last scanned at O'Hare" is a status update, not acknowledgment, and without acknowledgment wording elsewhere in the message the rating is Developing. Acknowledgment wording that fabricates or misreads the customer's stated concern (e.g., sympathizing about money when the customer said they only care about speed) also does not count — rate Developing

When the customer is calm, neutral, or cooperative: do not demand explicit empathy. Professional warmth earns Adequate; Strong requires genuinely skilled emotional work (e.g., proactively reassuring about an earlier concern); Developing only if the tone is cold, dismissive, or mishandles the customer.

Accurate reflection of the customer's concern and stakes supports a higher rating; empathetic wording that misunderstands the concern cannot be Strong. Providing status updates, facts, or solutions is task content and NEVER counts as emotional acknowledgment, no matter how useful the information is — acknowledgment requires wording that names or reflects the customer's emotion or situation.

Emotion feedback: one sentence, max 25 words, second person ("you", "your"). If Developing or Adequate: the single most important thing to do differently, specific to this turn and customer. If Strong: the strength to maintain. Cover interpersonal handling only — acknowledgment, tone, wording, timing — never procedural steps.
